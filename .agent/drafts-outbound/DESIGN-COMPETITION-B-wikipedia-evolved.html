Co-location Tier Methodology

Last reviewed 2026-05-22 · 8 contributors · 14,200 words

This article is also available in Spanish: Metodología de niveles de co-ubicación. For the parent topic, see Co-location Intelligence.

The Woodfine co-location tier methodology classifies retail commercial nodes by the density and diversity of anchor tenant categories, civic service adjacency, and estimated trade area population. The system operates across 13 countries and approximately 5,700 indexed clusters as of May 2026.1

Overview

The methodology was developed internally at Woodfine Capital Projects to provide a consistent classification basis for commercial nodes across heterogeneous national land-use regimes. Earlier industry approaches relied on raw gross leasable area or anchor count alone; the Woodfine system layers anchor diversity, civic adjacency, and population catchment into a composite score.

Each indexed cluster is assigned a tier from 1 (Anchor Composite) through 3 (Community Cluster). The classification is reviewed on a rolling 18-month cadence and validated against ground-truth footfall data where available.2

Methodology note
Tier assignments are intended to support internal portfolio analytics and are not used directly to determine transaction pricing. Forward-looking applications of the methodology carry BCSC NI 51-102 disclosure language.

Tier Definitions

The system distinguishes three tiers. Tier boundaries are defined empirically rather than by fixed population or anchor thresholds — cluster scores are normalised within each national market before tiering, accounting for differences in retail density baselines.

Tier 1 — Anchor Composite

Tier 1 clusters host a composite of at least four distinct anchor categories (grocery, mass merchandise, pharmacy, civic services) within a 600-metre walking radius. As of May 2026, 27 metropolitan markets contain at least one Tier 1 cluster, with the densest concentrations observed in Toronto, Madrid, and Mexico City.

Tier 2 — Regional Node

Tier 2 clusters retain anchor diversity but at lower density, and typically serve a regional catchment of 60,000–180,000 residents. These nodes form the operational backbone of the Woodfine portfolio model.

Tier 3 — Community Cluster

Tier 3 clusters serve smaller communities and are characterised by one or two anchors plus a stable convenience tail. These clusters are tracked but are not the focus of the firm's principal investment workstreams.

Data Sources

The tier methodology draws on three primary data substrates: anchor tenant location registries (proprietary, refreshed quarterly), public-sector population grids (WorldPop, US LODES, MITMA ES),3 and civic facility registries derived from OpenStreetMap with manual correction passes.

Anchor registries are maintained as an internal write-only ledger (WORM substrate); changes are appended and tied to a review timestamp, with prior values retained for audit. This posture supports BCSC continuous-disclosure traceability for any methodology output that subsequently surfaces in public-facing materials.

Catchment Methodology

Trade area estimation uses a hybrid drive-time and walk-time buffer, weighted by the proportion of working-age population within each band. The methodology does not assume a single modal mix; instead, modal weights are calibrated per-market using national travel survey data where available, and interpolated from peer markets otherwise.

Catchment estimates are produced at quarterly cadence and stored alongside the cluster record. Material methodology changes trigger re-tiering of all affected clusters and are noted in the CHANGELOG attached to each cluster's article.

Limitations

The methodology has three known limitations. First, the anchor diversity metric assumes that category labels translate cleanly across national markets, which is imperfect — e.g. the distinction between a hypermarché and a supermarket varies materially between France and Spain. Second, population grids lag actual demographic change by 2–4 years depending on the source. Third, civic facility registries derived from OpenStreetMap exhibit completeness variation by country.

Ongoing work targets reducing population grid lag through integration of utility-meter density signals and mobile-network presence data, subject to BCSC privacy posture review.
