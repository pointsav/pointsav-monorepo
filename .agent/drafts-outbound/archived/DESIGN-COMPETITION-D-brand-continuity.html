Co-location Tier Methodology

Reviewed · May 2026 8 min read Editorial Desk

The Woodfine co-location tier methodology classifies retail commercial nodes by the density and diversity of anchor tenant categories, civic service adjacency, and estimated trade area population. The system operates across 13 countries and approximately 5,700 indexed clusters as of May 2026.

Overview

A tier expresses the readiness of a cluster to host direct-hold retail space. It is not a credit score, a price signal, or a forward yield. The tier is a structural reading of three jointly necessary conditions: a sufficient anchor base, a population catchment capable of sustaining repeat trade, and a civic-service envelope that anchors weekday foot traffic independent of the discretionary cycle.

The methodology has been in continuous use, with periodic recalibration, since the 2021 cohort. The current calibration was finalised in the second quarter of 2026, drawing on the WorldPop 2026 population grid[1], the United States LODES 2022 employment release[2], and the MITMA Spain 2023 mobility dataset[3].

Where source data are silent, no tier is published. Imputation is not used to fill data gaps; the silence is itself the disclosure.

Tier definitions

Four tiers exist. Each is defined by joint thresholds — failing any one disqualifies the higher tier. The model is intentionally conjunctive; a single strong dimension does not compensate for a weak one.

A tier reading is a reading of the place, not of the deal. The same cluster will return the same tier whether or not Woodfine is the prospective acquirer.

Data sources

The methodology aggregates three categories of input: tenancy data licensed from regional retail intelligence providers, civic-service registries published by competent national authorities, and population catchments derived from open population grids.

Tenancy data are refreshed quarterly. Civic-service registries are refreshed annually where the national authority publishes on an annual cycle. Population grids are refreshed as published; the latest WorldPop release is 2026, the latest LODES vintage is 2022.

Where a national civic-service registry is not maintained on a published cycle, Woodfine relies on OpenStreetMap and Overture Maps adjudicated tags, with hand-review of the top two hundred clusters in the affected geography on a rolling 24-month cadence.

Refresh cadence

A cluster's tier may change between refresh cycles only on the basis of a verifiable on-the-ground change — a new anchor opening, a closure, or a published civic-service relocation. Algorithmic recalibration is logged separately and never retroactively applied.

Catchment methodology

Catchment is computed as the population residing within a network-distance polygon, parameterised by the dominant transit mode of the surrounding postal sector. In car-dominated sectors the catchment is a 12-minute drive isochrone. In transit-dominated sectors it is a 20-minute walk-plus-transit isochrone. In mixed sectors, both are computed and the union taken.

The isochrone is built on the latest published road-and-transit network for the country — typically OSM with national-authority overlays. The catchment value reported is the count of residents whose home address-grid centroid falls within the polygon, rounded to the nearest thousand.

Tourist and seasonal populations are excluded. Catchments are residential. Where a cluster's anchor mix is materially tourism-driven, the cluster carries a "tourism-weighted" annotation and is not assigned a tier under the standard methodology.

Limitations

The methodology is structural. It does not forecast same-store sales, predict tenant default, or imply a yield. It cannot detect changes between refresh cycles in jurisdictions where civic data are sparse, and it does not attempt to.

A tier reading is necessary but never sufficient for an acquisition decision. Underwriting requires lease-level review, environmental review, and a separate capital-plan assessment — none of which are inputs to the tier.

Users of the methodology are reminded that it is a reading instrument, not a recommendation instrument. The taxonomy is offered as a public reference; the decisions Woodfine takes on the basis of it are not.
