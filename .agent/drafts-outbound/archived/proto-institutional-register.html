Economic Model

This article describes the economic architecture of the PointSav platform. For the legal entities behind it, see Corporate Structure. For the operational mechanics by which work is admitted to the record, see Stage 6 Promotion.

The economic architecture of the PointSav platform is structured around three tiers: Vendor (PointSav Digital Systems), Customer (enterprise licensees), and Deployment (sovereign instances on customer infrastructure). Each tier is a separate ledger, with separate identities, separate signing keys, and a one-way commit flow between them. Reversing that flow is, by structure, not possible.

This article describes how the model operates in practice. It is intended to be read alongside the Constitutional Doctrine and the Three-Ring Architecture article, both of which it depends upon. Where this article and DOCTRINE.md disagree, DOCTRINE.md is authoritative; the conflict is to be surfaced as a cleanup item in NEXT.md.

§ 1The Three Tiers

The platform’s commercial substrate has exactly three tiers, and they do not commute. Vendor produces; Customer licenses; Deployment runs. There is no fourth tier, no recursive nesting, and no path by which an instance can write upstream to its vendor of record.

Vendor — PointSav Digital Systems

The vendor tier is canonical. It is the source of every artifact published under PointSav’s name — engineering code, doctrine, design system, conventions, and the wiki you are presently reading. Work enters this tier only by ratified promotion from a staging archive, and only with an SSH-signed commit attributable to a named human contributor.

Customer — Enterprise Licensees

The customer tier is a guide catalog. It contains operational runbooks, deployment manifests, and the bilingual READMEs that travel with a licensed instance. Customer-tier artifacts reference the vendor tier’s canonical artifacts by version; they do not duplicate them.

Deployment — Sovereign Instances

Each deployment is a numbered instance on customer hardware. The instance carries its own MANIFEST, its own continuous-disclosure record, and its own signing keys. The vendor never holds operational credentials for a deployed instance. This is the architectural posture that makes the platform suitable for buyers who require an air-gap.

§ 2Continuous-Disclosure Posture

The platform is built to a disclosure standard, not a marketing standard. Every artifact is reviewable under National Instrument 51‑102 and OSC Staff Notice 51‑721. Forward-looking material carries the qualifying language — planned, intended, may, target — throughout. The Sovereign Data Foundation is described in those terms exclusively; the platform makes no claim of regulatory approval it has not received.

PointSav builds operating systems and services for regulated businesses that need to own their data, their AI, and their record-keeping outright. The platform runs on customer hardware, produces continuous-disclosure-grade records by structure, and operates fully without AI for buyers that require an air-gap. — Product lede, public‑facing materials

The phrase “by structure” is load-bearing. Disclosure quality is not a feature toggle; it is a property of the architecture. A worm-ledger commit is signed, append-only, and reviewable; an unsigned commit cannot enter the canonical tier at all. The standard is enforced by the pre-commit identity gate, not by editorial discretion.

§ 3The Air-Gap Mode

A buyer who requires complete operational independence from generative AI infrastructure can run the platform fully without it. The deployment ships with the AI substrate disabled at the package level; downstream services degrade gracefully to deterministic equivalents. There is no hidden dependency on a remote inference endpoint, and no path by which one is silently installed.

This is a structural commitment rather than a marketing claim. It exists because customers in the financial, health, and legal sectors require a defensible answer to the question: What happens if the model provider goes offline, raises prices, or changes its terms? The answer the platform gives is: nothing — the deployment continues to operate.

§ 4What This Model Is Not

This document avoids three framings that the platform deliberately does not adopt.

  • It is not a SaaS revenue model. The vendor does not collect telemetry, does not aggregate customer data, and does not derive revenue from usage of the deployed instance after license.
  • It is not a network-effects model. Each instance is self-contained. The platform does not become more valuable as more customers run it; it becomes more credible, but the value to any single customer is intrinsic.
  • It is not a competitive-comparison model. The platform is described in structural terms throughout. Comparisons with named vendors are not present in this article or any other article of record.
