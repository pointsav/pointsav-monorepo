The Economic Model: Three Tiers, One Ledger

How vendor, customer, and deployment compose into a self-evidencing economic record — and why a three-tier separation is the precondition for sovereign software in regulated industries.

By The PointSav Engineering Office · Published 14 May 2026 · Revised 25 May 2026 · Reading time 14 minutes · Citations 11

Contents

  1. The Three Tiers
  2. Vendor: PointSav Digital Systems
  3. Customer: Enterprise Licensees
  4. Deployment: Sovereign Instances
  5. The Flow of Records
  6. Why Three, Not Two
  7. A Note on Pricing
  8. Implications for Regulators

The economic architecture of the PointSav platform is structured around three tiers: Vendor (PointSav Digital Systems), Customer (enterprise licensees), and Deployment (sovereign instances on customer infrastructure). It is not a service hierarchy. It is a separation of economic actors, each with a distinct ledger, distinct obligations, and distinct ownership of records. The arrangement exists because regulated businesses cannot operate inside a vendor's tenant — they must operate against the vendor at arm's length, in writing, on hardware they control.

For a financial services firm, a hospital network, or a law firm subject to provincial regulators, the question is not whether the software performs the work. It is whether the record of the work will survive contact with an auditor, a litigant, a successor in interest, or the regulator. The three-tier model is the structural answer to that question.

Section One The Three Tiers

The platform separates the production of software (vendor) from the consumption of software (customer) from the operation of software (deployment). Each tier maintains its own canonical record. The vendor's record is the source-of-truth for what the software is; the customer's record is the source-of-truth for what they licensed; the deployment's record is the source-of-truth for what was operated. The three records are linked but not merged.

This is not a technical choice — it is a legal one. A vendor whose ledger contains customer operational data has, by inadvertence, become a custodian. A customer whose ledger contains deployment internals has, by inadvertence, taken on operational liability for systems they do not run. The separation forces each actor's obligations to remain visible to that actor alone, and to whatever downstream parties they explicitly admit.

The vendor sells a guide. The customer publishes a guide. The deployment follows a guide. Three records, three actors, one chain of evidence. Doctrine §III, Customer-First Ordering

Section Two Vendor: PointSav Digital Systems

The vendor tier is a single GitHub organisation — github.com/pointsav — under the legal entity PointSav Digital Systems, a subsidiary of Woodfine Capital Projects Inc. The vendor's record is software, documentation, and the engineering library you are presently reading. The vendor does not store customer data, does not operate customer systems, and does not receive customer telemetry.

The vendor's commercial product is licensed source code, supported by the published documentation. The vendor's incentive structure is aligned with software durability rather than with usage volume: there are no per-seat fees, no per-transaction metering, and no phone-home licence checks. A customer who installs a release at year-zero and never speaks to the vendor again is operating the product correctly. The vendor's invoice is for the document, not for the service.

The vendor's three obligations

The vendor commits to three things, recorded in the engineering library and visible to anyone who reads it:

  1. A canonical record. Every published release is tagged, signed, and accompanied by a changelog. The record is durable across vendor turnover.
  2. An air-gappable build. Every release builds without network access. The vendor cannot insert telemetry that the customer cannot disable at the source.
  3. A bilingual library. All public-facing documentation is maintained in English and Spanish; operational documentation is English-only by deliberate choice.

Section Three Customer: Enterprise Licensees

The customer tier — a separate GitHub organisation, github.com/woodfine for the reference customer — holds the catalogue of operational guides. A guide is a customer-authored document that describes how the customer intends to run a piece of vendor software. The customer's record is the licensee's interpretation of the vendor's documentation; it is the trail that a regulator would follow to understand what the customer chose to do with the product.

The distinction matters. A vendor's documentation states what the software can do. A customer's guide states what the customer's organisation requires it to do. The two documents are different artifacts. A customer with no guide is a customer who has not yet decided what they require — a position that is acceptable in early procurement but unacceptable in audit.

The vendor's documentation states what the software can do. The customer's guide states what the customer's organisation requires it to do.

The guide as a procurement artifact

A guide is the procurement artifact that survives the procurement decision. It is bound to the customer's regulatory posture, not the vendor's product roadmap. When a customer's compliance officer is asked, two years after a deployment, why the system was configured in a particular way, the answer is the guide — not a vendor support ticket, not a recorded call, not a Slack message.

Section Four Deployment: Sovereign Instances

The deployment tier is the actual running instance. It lives on customer infrastructure — physical, virtual, or otherwise — and carries a deployment manifest that records its provenance: which vendor release, which customer guide, which operator account, which hardware. The deployment is not tracked in the customer's GitHub organisation; it is a local artifact, gitignored from the catalogue repository, but referenced by manifest.

The deployment is where the system produces records — journals, ledgers, audit trails, operational logs. Those records belong to the customer, not to the vendor and not to any intermediary. The deployment manifest declares this explicitly, and the vendor's licence text disclaims any right to the records the deployment produces.

A deployment is a sovereign instance. The customer owns the hardware, the records, the keys, and the right to walk away. The vendor's only continuing role is to publish further releases the customer may, at their option, install. Doctrine §IV.a, Deployment Sovereignty

Section Five The Flow of Records

Records flow in one direction only — from vendor to customer to deployment. There is no reverse flow. The vendor does not pull telemetry from deployments. The customer does not pull deployment logs back into the catalogue. The catalogue does not pull documentation back into the vendor's repositories. Each tier writes forward; each tier reads backward.

The forward-only flow is a structural property, not a policy. It is enforced by the topology of the repositories themselves, by the absence of credentials in the upstream direction, and by the design of the deployment manifest, which lists only its predecessors and never its successors.

Section Six Why Three, Not Two

A simpler design would collapse vendor and customer — a single repository for the source of truth — or collapse customer and deployment — a single record of "the running system." Each simplification is more common in practice. Each is incorrect for regulated capital. The reasoning is set out at length in the Three-Ring Architecture article, but in summary:

  • Collapsing vendor and customer puts the vendor inside the customer's regulatory perimeter. The vendor becomes a regulated party whether or not it is in the business of regulation.
  • Collapsing customer and deployment removes the procurement artifact. The compliance officer has no document, only a server state. Server states are not testimony.
  • Three tiers is the smallest number that produces a written record of intent, a written record of choice, and a written record of execution — without merging any two.

Section Seven A Note on Pricing

Pricing is set at the customer tier, not at the vendor tier. The vendor publishes its licence text; the customer negotiates terms based on the breadth of guides it intends to publish and the number of deployments it intends to operate. The vendor does not have visibility into the customer's deployment count, and does not require it for invoicing purposes.

This produces an unusual artifact: a vendor invoice that lists deliverables — published guides, training engagements, source-code licences — and not consumption. The invoice is the bill of materials, not the bill of usage. Customers report that this alone is a reason to switch.

Section Eight Implications for Regulators

The arrangement produces a documentary trail that a regulator can follow without speaking to the vendor. The vendor's library establishes what the software is; the customer's catalogue establishes what the customer required of it; the deployment's manifest establishes what was actually operated. Each layer is inspectable in writing, in English (and where applicable, Spanish), on infrastructure the regulated party controls.

PointSav offers no assurance that any specific regulator will accept this arrangement. That is a matter for the customer's counsel and the regulator's discretion. The arrangement is, however, designed to make the conversation easier than the alternative, in which the regulated party must explain why a third party holds custody of their operational records.

The continuous-disclosure posture described in BCSC Continuous-Disclosure Posture is the writing discipline that follows from this structural choice. The two articles should be read together.
